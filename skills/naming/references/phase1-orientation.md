# Phase 1 — Orientation

Build a naming brief before generating candidates. Ask only for missing information; use one focused
question at a time when the user wants guidance, or a compact questionnaire when they request speed.

## Required Decisions

1. **Type** — What is being named: company, product, feature, screen, design system, component
   library, campaign, or another creative project?
2. **Purpose** — What specifically breaks or remains difficult if it does not exist?
3. **Promise** — What happens in the first few minutes of use? Keep the claim concrete enough to
   survive contact with reality.
4. **Audience** — Who matters most, in what context, and which adjacent brands do they admire?
5. **Feeling** — Select one dominant emotional result, such as relief, momentum, status, wonder,
   rebellion, belonging, or control.
6. **Tone** — Place the name on three sliders: playful–serious, accessible–exclusive, and
   innovative–traditional.
7. **Environment** — Record competitors, crowded name types, open territory, legal or cultural
   concerns, spelling constraints, and stakeholder non-starters.
8. **Range** — Decide whether the name must support future products, markets, or organizational use.

For design systems and component libraries, clarify whether the name should signal the company
brand or the system's function. The primary audience is usually internal designers and developers,
and the desired feeling often leans toward control or momentum; confirm rather than assume this.

## Output

Produce a concise naming brief:

```markdown
## Naming brief

- Thing and type:
- Purpose:
- Immediate promise:
- Primary audience and context:
- Dominant feeling:
- Tone: playful–serious / accessible–exclusive / innovative–traditional
- Competitive opening:
- Non-starters and constraints:
- Required future range:
```

Phase 1 is complete when every field contains a specific answer or an explicitly labeled assumption.
